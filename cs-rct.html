Research Question & Estimand

Identifying the Causal Effect of Adaptive Session Pacing on Retention

The platform from Case Study 01 wanted to test a product change: replacing fixed 45-minute sessions with an adaptive pacing system that reads engagement signals and adjusts session length in real time. The obvious analysis — comparing outcomes across students who happened to use the feature — wouldn't work. Students who engage more are already different in the ways that predict retention.

So we ran an experiment. Before touching any outcome data, we filed a pre-registration specifying the estimand, the randomization scheme, the target sample size, and the criterion that would trigger a deployment recommendation. Primary estimand: ATT. Secondary: ITT. Analysis locked before unblinding.

Power Analysis

Minimum Detectable Effect and Sample Size Determination

The outcome was 90-day retention, binary. With a baseline rate of 71% from the prior 18-month cohort and a two-sided test at α = 0.05 with 80% power, we needed to detect a 5 percentage point lift. That threshold was set by the client — below 5 pp the feature wouldn't recover its development cost.

From those parameters, the normal approximation to the binomial gives n ≈ 604 per arm. We added a 15% attrition buffer and targeted 700, or 1,400 total. Final enrollment: 1,412 (706 per arm) over a 12-week window.

Running the sensitivity analysis across MDEs from 3 pp to 8 pp made the constraint concrete: effects below 3 pp would require more than 2,200 students per arm — longer than the platform's product cycle allowed.

Randomization & Covariate Balance

Blocked Assignment and Pre-Treatment Balance Verification

Assignment was stratified on two variables: enrollment cohort month and subscription tier. Cohort month controlled for seasonal retention patterns; tier controlled for a known confound between plan type and engagement levels. Within each stratum, students were assigned 50/50 using a seeded CSPRNG, with the seed committed to the pre-registration document before any assignments were made.

After enrollment closed, we checked balance across eight pre-treatment covariates using standardized mean differences. The largest SMD was 0.08 — well below the 0.10 threshold. A KS test on baseline session frequency returned p = 0.61.

Blocking on two variables roughly halved estimator variance relative to simple random assignment — equivalent to needing about 250 fewer students to hit the same power target.

Variance Reduction

CUPED and Covariate Adjustment for Precision Improvement

CUPED reduced variance further at the analysis stage. The covariate vector was: 30-day session frequency from the two months before enrollment, baseline drop-off score, and the at-risk flag from the churn model in Case Study 01. Together they explained R² = 0.31 of outcome variance.

R² = 0.31 translates to a 31% variance reduction, narrowing the 95% CI by about 18% versus the unadjusted difference-in-means. The same precision we got with 1,412 students would have required roughly 1,860 without CUPED.

Primary Analysis

Frequentist Hypothesis Test and Causal Effect Estimate

The primary analysis was a two-sample z-test on CUPED-adjusted outcomes under ITT. The pre-registration specified two conditions for rejecting the null: p < 0.05, and a lower confidence bound above 2 pp. Both conditions had to hold.

Retention was 76.8% in the treatment arm and 71.4% in the control arm. The CUPED-adjusted estimate is +5.4 pp (z = 4.06, p < 0.001), with a 95% CI lower bound of +2.8 pp. Both pre-registered criteria were satisfied.

Heterogeneous Treatment Effects

Bayesian Updating and BART Subgroup Analysis

As a secondary analysis we fit a Bayesian beta-binomial model: P(τ > 0) = 0.97. The more interesting secondary result came from BART. We used the churn model's at-risk score to stratify students into quintiles. The gradient is steep: students in the top risk quintile show a posterior mean effect of +9.1 pp [+5.8, +12.4], while the bottom quintile shows +1.8 pp [−1.1, +4.7].

Targeting quintiles Q3–Q5 would capture roughly 85% of the estimated retention gain while reaching only 60% of students. That's the rollout we recommended.
