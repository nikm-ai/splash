Problem Statement

Predicting 90-Day Churn from Behavioral Engagement Signals

A tutoring platform was losing students at a rate its support team couldn't see coming. By the time a cancellation showed up, the window for intervention had already closed. The work here was to build a churn classifier that would surface at-risk students early enough to act on, and rank them by probability so a team with limited bandwidth could focus where it mattered most.

The intervention constraint shaped every decision downstream. A model optimized for accuracy would flag too few students. A model optimized for recall with no budget would flood the outreach team. The operating point had to match the team's weekly capacity — roughly 15% of the active student population.

Feature Engineering

Behavioral Signal Construction

All features were derived from raw session and assessment event logs, with no demographic or subscription-tier data included. The feature set needed to capture how engagement was changing, not who a student was at enrollment.

Model Development

XGBoost Training and Threshold Optimization

XGBoost was selected after 5-fold cross-validation against logistic regression and random forest. Recall was the primary metric, not accuracy. Missing an at-risk student costs more than an unnecessary outreach call, and the model threshold needed to reflect that asymmetry.

Threshold selection was done on the validation set against the precision-recall curve, targeting the operating point where the flagged population matched what the outreach team could realistically contact in a week: roughly 15% of active students.

Deployment Architecture

Building the Outreach Prioritization Pipeline

The model runs as a weekly batch job rather than a real-time endpoint, which fits the support team's Monday-morning workflow. Each scoring run ranks all active students by churn probability and surfaces the top three contributing features per student, so outreach staff know why someone is flagged, not just that they are.

  • Weekly batch inference on all active students with tenure under 12 months
  • Top 15% of the scored population flagged for high-priority outreach
  • Students in months 1–3 carry a separate cohort flag given their elevated baseline hazard
  • Model retrained quarterly on a rolling 18-month window as engagement patterns shift
Time-to-contact for at-risk students dropped from 14 days to 4 days in the first quarter after launch.
The 81% recall threshold was set to match outreach capacity, not to maximize model performance. At higher thresholds the model flags more true positives, but the team runs out of bandwidth. That tradeoff is documented explicitly as a staffing constraint, not a model limitation.

The ~8% figure should be treated as an observational estimate. There was no formal holdout control group, and other operational changes happened over the same period. The number is directionally plausible but causally unverified. A randomized outreach experiment is the right next step — see Case Study 06 for that work.
