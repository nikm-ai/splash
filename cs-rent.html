Problem Statement

Systematic Mispricing in Residential Portfolio Management

At scale, manual rent-setting produces two predictable inefficiencies: units priced above market sit vacant, and units priced below market leave revenue uncollected. Neither shows up obviously in the data unless you have a reference point for what rent should be.

We trained a gradient boosting model to predict market-clearing rent from observable property and neighborhood features, then used the signed gap between prediction and asking price as the pricing signal. The outputs were surfaced through a Looker dashboard built for property managers with no quantitative background.

Feature Set

Observable Listing and Market-Level Predictors

All features came from the client's property management system, comparable transaction data, and public census variables. No proprietary third-party feeds were used — the client had a hard constraint on data vendor spend, and the model needed to be reproducible on their existing infrastructure.

Model Performance

Gradient Boosting versus Ridge Regression Baseline

A Ridge regression baseline was trained on the same features to establish a linear benchmark. The GBM outperformed it most in the premium segment, where the relationship between rent, location, and demand is non-linear and Ridge can only approximate.

The 14.6 pp gain over Ridge came from three places the linear model can't handle:

  • Non-linear distance decay (premium units closer to center command disproportionately higher rent)
  • Segment-by-market interaction effects not expressible in a linear form
  • Threshold effects in the demand score at high-demand submarkets
At $164/month MAE on a median rent around $2,200, the model's error is small enough that a 5% deviation threshold catches real pricing gaps rather than noise. That calibration was deliberate.
Mispricing Detection

Translating Model Estimates into Pricing Decisions

A listing was flagged as mispriced if its asking rent deviated from the model estimate by more than 5% in either direction. That threshold was set against the model's own error distribution: flagging gaps smaller than ~$110 on a $2,200 unit would mostly flag prediction noise, not real pricing problems.

  • Overpriced (above threshold): flag for rent reduction at next renewal opportunity
  • Underpriced (below threshold): flag for rent increase; demand score considered before recommendation
  • Within band: no action recommended; monitor quarterly

Dashboard & Handoff

Looker Dashboard for Non-Technical Property Managers

The dashboard was built for property managers with no quantitative background. The design principle was simple: show the conclusion, not the model. Each listing displays a status (overpriced, underpriced, or within band), the recommended dollar adjustment, and an estimated occupancy impact. The model mechanics are not exposed.

  • Revenue impact expressed as annualized dollars — not percentages — per manager feedback during testing
  • Demand score shown as a percentile rank within submarket, not a raw score
  • Model version and last-refresh timestamp displayed on every view to maintain trust in data currency
Within a month of launch, property managers were making repricing decisions independently, without looping in the analytics team. That was the stated adoption goal, and it happened faster than expected.
